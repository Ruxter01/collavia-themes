Understanding the DEAL Document

A comprehensive guide to using the DEAL document for effective sales follow-up and opportunity management.

Philosophy of DEAL

Most of the buying process happens in the time between meetings. A strong meeting can create clarity and momentum, but that momentum will fade if it is not reinforced. The buyer leaves the conversation and has to interpret what was discussed, align stakeholders, and determine what to do next. DEAL exists to support that part of the process. It is a structured way to: Preserve alignment from the meeting, Clarify responsibilities on both sides, Guide the buyer through their decision process. When used well, DEAL becomes a shared working document that evolves with the opportunity. It helps the buyer move forward with clarity and confidence, while giving you visibility into whether the opportunity is progressing. This is a core part of enabling buying.

What Is a DEAL Document?

A DEAL document is a structured follow-up that captures: What matters to the buyer, What needs to happen next, Who is responsible for each step, What success looks like. It is designed to maintain alignment and create forward movement between conversations.

A DEAL document is a structured follow-up that captures:

  • What needs to happen next
  • Who is responsible for each step
  • What success looks like

It is designed to maintain alignment and create forward movement between conversations.

When to Use DEAL

Use DEAL after any meaningful sales conversation where: A problem or opportunity has been identified, There is interest in continuing the conversation, Next steps are required to move forward. Your first DEAL should typically follow the first strong meeting. From there, the document should be updated and refined after key interactions. A proposal should not be created until there is strong alignment within the DEAL document.

Mechanics of a DEAL Document

A DEAL document has four components: D – Determine Requirements, E – Engage the Prospect, A – Assume Responsibility, L – List Desired Outcomes. Each section should be clearly labeled and use numbered bullets. This format is critical. Numbered bullets make the document easy to scan and easy to respond to. A buyer can reference specific points such as "D2" or "A3," which makes communication faster and more precise.

Using DEAL to Drive Momentum

DEAL is not a one-time document. It is iterative. Each version should reflect the current state of the opportunity, incorporate feedback from the buyer, and clarify what has changed and what remains. One of the most important signals to watch is engagement. Prioritize the opportunities where the buyer is actively engaging. These are far more likely to move forward.

DEAL is not a one-time document. It is iterative.

Each version should:

  • Reflect the current state of the opportunity
  • Incorporate feedback from the buyer
  • Clarify what has changed and what remains

This process helps you:

  • Build alignment over time
  • Surface misunderstandings early
  • Guide the buyer through their decision process

One of the most important signals to watch is engagement.

  • Some prospects respond quickly, provide feedback, and engage with the content
  • Others do not respond or provide minimal input

This is a strong buying signal. Prioritize the opportunities where the buyer is actively engaging.

DEAL as a Qualification and Management Tool

DEAL is not just a communication tool. It is also a way to evaluate opportunity quality. Strong opportunities show clear requirements, active engagement, defined responsibilities, and consistent forward movement. From a management perspective, DEAL can serve as a valuable KPI.

Strong opportunities typically show:

  • Clear and accurate requirements
  • Active engagement from the buyer
  • Defined responsibilities on both sides
  • Consistent forward movement

Weaker opportunities often show:

  • Vague or incomplete requirements
  • Little or no engagement with the DEAL document
  • Unclear next steps
  • Inconsistent follow-through

From a management perspective, DEAL can also serve as a valuable KPI:

  • Number of DEAL documents created
  • Quality and completeness of each section
  • Level of buyer engagement and response

Warning Signs

Be cautious if you see no response to a DEAL document, minimal or vague feedback, lack of ownership in the Engage the Prospect section, or repeated delays and unclear next steps. These often indicate a lack of alignment or a weak opportunity.

  • Minimal or vague feedback

D – Determine Requirements

This section captures what matters most to the buyer.

  • Problem-focused (What is not working, what needs to be fixed)
  • Goal-focused (What they are trying to achieve)

Problem-focused language is often more powerful because it reinforces urgency and impact.

  • Capture direct quotes from your notes or transcripts
  • Use their wording to describe the problem or goal
  • Include specific numbers or metrics

E – Engage the Prospect

This section defines what the buyer needs to do to move the opportunity forward. This is where buying activity becomes visible.

A – Assume Responsibility

This section outlines what you will do to support the buyer. It demonstrates structure, credibility, and commitment.

L – List Desired Outcomes

This section defines what success looks like at this stage of the opportunity.

Sample DEAL Document

DEAL 1 (After Initial Meeting)

A. Your Requirements

  1. You are currently onboarding new clients in an inconsistent and largely manual way, with timelines ranging from 45 to 75 days
  2. Delays are most often occurring between contract signature and project kickoff
  3. This is creating a negative first impression for new clients
  4. Your leadership team has identified onboarding as a priority area for improvement this year
  5. There is currently limited visibility into where clients are in the onboarding process
  6. You are looking for a more structured and scalable onboarding approach

B. Your Next Steps

  1. Review and confirm that the requirements outlined above accurately reflect your current situation
  2. Identify additional stakeholders from operations and client success
  3. Schedule a follow-up meeting with those stakeholders

C. Our Next Steps

  1. Prepare examples of how similar organizations have improved onboarding timelines
  2. Identify initial areas where process structure could reduce delays
  3. Bring a subject matter expert to the next meeting

D. Desired Outcomes

  1. Agreement that the onboarding process is a priority area to address
  2. Alignment on the core challenges contributing to delays
  3. Confirmation of next steps and stakeholder involvement

DEAL 2 (After Feedback and Second Meeting)

A. Your Requirements

  1. Confirmed that onboarding timelines are averaging approximately 65 days, with a goal to reduce to 30 days or less
  2. Inconsistency across teams is a primary driver of delays
  3. Lack of defined ownership during key stages is contributing to missed handoffs
  4. Limited visibility into onboarding progress is preventing proactive management
  5. Operations and client success leaders are aligned on the impact of improving onboarding

B. Your Next Steps

  1. Provide access to current onboarding workflow documentation
  2. Confirm availability of operations and client success leads for a working session
  3. Share any existing metrics or KPIs used to track onboarding performance

C. Our Next Steps

  1. Review onboarding workflow and identify key inefficiencies
  2. Develop a high-level improvement approach focused on speed, consistency, and visibility
  3. Share relevant case examples with quantified results

D. Desired Outcomes

  1. Alignment on the root causes of onboarding delays
  2. Agreement on priority areas for improvement
  3. Initial alignment on the direction of a potential solution
