Following Up with DEAL

Philosophy

Most of the buying process happens in the time between meetings.

A strong meeting can create clarity and momentum, but that momentum will fade if it is not reinforced. The buyer leaves the conversation and has to interpret what was discussed, align stakeholders, and determine what to do next.

DEAL exists to support that part of the process.

It is a structured way to:

  • Preserve alignment from the meeting
  • Clarify responsibilities on both sides
  • Guide the buyer through their decision process

When used well, DEAL becomes a shared working document that evolves with the opportunity. It helps the buyer move forward with clarity and confidence, while giving you visibility into whether the opportunity is progressing.

This is a core part of enabling buying.

What Is a DEAL Document?

A DEAL document is a structured follow-up that captures:

  • What matters to the buyer
  • What needs to happen next
  • Who is responsible for each step
  • What success looks like

It is designed to maintain alignment and create forward movement between conversations.

When to Use DEAL

Use DEAL after any meaningful sales conversation where:

  • A problem or opportunity has been identified
  • There is interest in continuing the conversation
  • Next steps are required to move forward

Your first DEAL should typically follow the first strong meeting.

From there, the document should be updated and refined after key interactions. It should evolve as:

  • The problem becomes clearer
  • Additional stakeholders are involved
  • The path forward takes shape

A proposal should not be created until there is strong alignment within the DEAL document.

Mechanics

A DEAL document has four components:

  • D – Determine Requirements
  • E – Engage the Prospect
  • A – Assume Responsibility
  • L – List Desired Outcomes

Each section should be clearly labeled and use numbered bullets.

This format is critical.

Numbered bullets make the document easy to scan and easy to respond to. A buyer can reference specific points such as "D2" or "A3," which makes communication faster and more precise.

This structure drives engagement as much as the content itself.

D – Determine Requirements

Back to DEAL Structure

This section captures what matters most to the buyer.

You can frame this in one of two ways:

  • Problem-focused (What is not working, what needs to be fixed)
  • Goal-focused (What they are trying to achieve)

Both are valid. You may also use a combination, but clarity is critical.

Problem-focused language is often more powerful because it reinforces urgency and impact.

This is one of the most effective places to use the buyer's exact language. When possible:

  • Capture direct quotes from your notes or transcripts
  • Use their wording to describe the problem or goal
  • Include specific numbers or metrics (for example, moving from X to Y, or a problem costing $X)

Your goal is to:

  • Distill what you heard into clear, concise points
  • Prioritize the most important issues
  • Reflect both business and personal impact where possible

This section should feel accurate and complete to the buyer. If it is incomplete or inaccurate, the opportunity is likely to stall until it is corrected.

E – Engage the Prospect

Back to DEAL Structure

This section defines what the buyer needs to do to move the opportunity forward.

This is where buying activity becomes visible.

Examples may include:

  • Confirming or refining the requirements
  • Involving additional stakeholders
  • Providing information or data
  • Participating in follow-up conversations

In early versions of a DEAL document, this section may be very light. For example:

  • Review and confirm the requirements
  • Schedule a meeting with additional stakeholders

As the opportunity develops, this section becomes more detailed. It reflects the work the buyer must do internally to make a decision.

Clarity in this section helps maintain momentum and ensures the opportunity continues to move forward.

A – Assume Responsibility

Back to DEAL Structure

This section outlines what you will do to support the buyer.

It demonstrates structure, credibility, and commitment.

Examples may include:

  • Bringing in subject matter experts
  • Preparing additional information or analysis
  • Sharing relevant success stories or references
  • Outlining potential approaches

Like the Engage the Prospect section, this evolves over time.

In early DEAL documents, your responsibilities may be simple:

  • Participate in the next meeting
  • Provide initial insights or resources

In later versions, this section often becomes more detailed and may begin to resemble a high-level outline of how you will approach solving the problem.

Together, the Engage the Prospect and Assume Responsibility sections define how the opportunity will move forward.

L – List Desired Outcomes

Back to DEAL Structure

This section defines what success looks like at this stage of the opportunity.

It answers the question: What needs to happen for this opportunity to move forward in a meaningful way?

This section is often the shortest, especially early in the process.

In early DEAL documents, desired outcomes may include:

  • Agreement on the requirements
  • Involvement of additional stakeholders
  • Alignment on next steps

As the opportunity develops, this section should evolve to reflect deeper alignment, such as:

  • Agreement on the problem and approach
  • Clarity on priorities
  • Alignment on timing
  • Alignment on a general level of investment

Over time, this section should reflect the progress the buyer is making toward a decision and the key milestones required to get there.

Using DEAL to Drive Momentum

DEAL is not a one-time document. It is iterative.

Each version should:

  • Reflect the current state of the opportunity
  • Incorporate feedback from the buyer
  • Clarify what has changed and what remains

This process helps you:

  • Build alignment over time
  • Surface misunderstandings early
  • Guide the buyer through their decision process

One of the most important signals to watch is engagement.

If you send multiple DEAL documents across opportunities, patterns will emerge:

  • Some prospects respond quickly, provide feedback, and engage with the content
  • Others do not respond or provide minimal input

This is a strong buying signal.

Prioritize the opportunities where the buyer is actively engaging. These are far more likely to move forward.

DEAL as a Qualification and Management Tool

DEAL is not just a communication tool. It is also a way to evaluate opportunity quality.

Strong opportunities typically show:

  • Clear and accurate requirements
  • Active engagement from the buyer
  • Defined responsibilities on both sides
  • Consistent forward movement

Weaker opportunities often show:

  • Vague or incomplete requirements
  • Little or no engagement with the DEAL document
  • Unclear next steps
  • Inconsistent follow-through

From a management perspective, DEAL can also serve as a valuable KPI:

  • Number of DEAL documents created
  • Quality and completeness of each section
  • Level of buyer engagement and response

These indicators provide insight into pipeline health and sales effectiveness.

Warning Signs

Be cautious if you see:

  • No response to a DEAL document
  • Minimal or vague feedback
  • Lack of ownership in the "Engage the Prospect" section
  • Repeated delays or unclear next steps

These often indicate a lack of alignment or a weak opportunity.

Sample DEAL Document

Below is a simplified example showing how a DEAL document may evolve over time.

DEAL 1 — After Initial Meeting

A. Your Requirements

  1. You are currently onboarding new clients in an inconsistent and largely manual way, with timelines ranging from 45 to 75 days depending on the team and client complexity
  2. Delays are most often occurring between contract signature and project kickoff, where ownership is unclear and handoffs are inconsistent
  3. This is creating a negative first impression for new clients, with several recent examples of clients expressing frustration during the onboarding phase
  4. Your leadership team has identified onboarding as a priority area for improvement this year, with a goal of reducing average onboarding time to approximately 30 days
  5. There is currently limited visibility into where clients are in the onboarding process, making it difficult to proactively manage timelines or identify bottlenecks
  6. You are looking for a more structured and scalable onboarding approach that can be applied consistently across teams without adding significant administrative burden

B. Your Next Steps

  1. Review and confirm that the requirements outlined above accurately reflect your current situation
  2. Identify additional stakeholders from operations and client success who should be involved in improving the onboarding process
  3. Schedule a follow-up meeting with those stakeholders to further explore the current workflow and challenges

C. Our Next Steps

  1. Prepare examples of how similar organizations have improved onboarding timelines and consistency
  2. Identify initial areas where process structure and visibility could reduce delays
  3. Bring a subject matter expert to the next meeting to provide additional perspective

D. Desired Outcomes

  1. Agreement that the onboarding process is a priority area to address
  2. Alignment on the core challenges contributing to delays
  3. Confirmation of next steps and stakeholder involvement

DEAL 2 — After Feedback and Second Meeting

A. Your Requirements

  1. Confirmed that onboarding timelines are averaging approximately 65 days, with a goal to reduce to 30 days or less
  2. Inconsistency across teams is a primary driver of delays, particularly during early-stage coordination and kickoff preparation
  3. Lack of defined ownership during key stages of onboarding is contributing to missed handoffs and rework
  4. Limited visibility into onboarding progress is preventing proactive management and delaying issue identification
  5. Operations and client success leaders are aligned that improving onboarding speed and consistency will have a direct impact on client satisfaction and retention

B. Your Next Steps

  1. Provide access to current onboarding workflow documentation and any existing process maps
  2. Confirm availability of operations and client success leads for a working session
  3. Share any existing metrics or KPIs used to track onboarding performance

C. Our Next Steps

  1. Review onboarding workflow and identify key inefficiencies and breakdown points
  2. Develop a high-level improvement approach focused on speed, consistency, and visibility
  3. Share relevant case examples with quantified results from similar organizations

D. Desired Outcomes

  1. Alignment on the root causes of onboarding delays
  2. Agreement on priority areas for improvement
  3. Initial alignment on the direction of a potential solution

Simple Version

  • DEAL maintains alignment between meetings
  • It defines what matters, what happens next, and who is responsible
  • Strong DEAL usage creates momentum and supports better decisions
  • Buyer engagement with the DEAL document is a strong signal of opportunity quality
